# ERIE — MUSEUM

## *the park is the gallery // the encounter is the work*

A decentralized, open-air, park-shaped museum and the symbiotic lab that makes it run.

---

## I. The Proposition

A museum is, traditionally, four walls and a ceiling. You buy a ticket. You enter a temperature-controlled room. You read a small label. You move on. The artist who made the work is not there. The community of strangers who happen to be standing next to you is incidental. The transaction between maker and audience has been outsourced to an institution that takes a cut, sets the lighting, decides who is shown, and closes at 5 p.m.

ERIE — MUSEUM is a different shape entirely.

It is a park — Washington Square is the model, but the form is portable to any well-loved public green — turned into a recurring, week-after-week, all-weather open-air gallery. Artists set up, work, show, and sell on the same path on which they walk their dogs. The audience is whoever happens to be in the park that day: students, retirees, parents with strollers, tourists, the chess players, the breakdancers, the people on a lunch break. The price of admission is the price of walking into a public square, which is to say nothing. The artist is present. The artist is not on the other side of a velvet rope. The artist is sitting on a folding stool, three feet away, willing to talk.

Two products live at every artist station. The first is the original — the one-of-one, the painting on canvas, the hand-bound book, the hand-thrown ceramic, the framed photograph, the unique sculpture. This is the museum. The second is the companion piece — a t-shirt, a hoodie, a tote, a cap, a printed scarf, a pin — co-designed with ERIE Labs and carrying the artist's image, name, and royalty. This is the museum shop. Both products live at the same table. The visitor chooses which they want — the art they will live with, or the souvenir they will wear into the rest of their week — and they pay the artist or the artist's collaborator directly.

The thesis is small and urgent: the encounter is the work. The museum is whatever holds the encounter. The garment is a piece of paper that carries the encounter into the rest of the wearer's life.

This codex defines the model. It explains what ERIE — MUSEUM is, how the partnership with ERIE Labs functions, why the park is the right shape for it, what the two-tier product architecture solves, how the artist gets paid, how the audience participates, and where the model goes in the next five years.

## II. The Name

**ERIE** carries forward from the lifestyle house — the inland-sea word, the *eerie* charge of weather changing, the moment-before-something-happens. The brand's character.

**MUSEUM** is used here in the deepest, oldest sense: from Greek *mouseion*, the seat of the Muses. Not a building. A *gathering place* for inspiration. The original *mouseion* in Alexandria was a research community — not a tourist attraction. The Muses were patrons of memory, music, dance, history, astronomy, comedy, tragedy, and erotic poetry. They lived everywhere people gathered with attention. ERIE — MUSEUM returns the word to that meaning.

The em-dash between the two words signals the same architectural pause that *ERIE — FUTURE* uses. Two halves; one held together by a deliberate breath.

The phrase is functional, not metaphorical. There is, literally, a museum. It just happens to be everywhere there are artists and audiences in the same square.

## III. The Park as the Right Shape

Why a park, and not a building, and not a website, and not a fair, and not a popup?

A park is a public good with five properties that no other space has all at once. It is **continuously open** — the museum has hours; the park does not. It is **socially mixed** — the audience is whoever happens to walk through, not whoever paid the admission, which means the museum reaches an audience that no curated institution can buy. It is **already permitted** — the park is built for gathering, eating, sitting, talking; the artist is one more reason to be there. It is **iconographically warm** — Washington Square has fountains, arches, trees, chess tables, dogs, music, the smell of food trucks, the rhythm of street performers; the museum borrows all of this for free as ambient design. And it is **memory-dense** — the park is where people walk on first dates, push strollers, smoke after exams, propose, mourn, celebrate; the work the visitor encounters there laminates into the rest of the visitor's life in ways a white-cube cannot replicate.

Washington Square Park, specifically, is the inaugural site for a reason. The park already runs the Conservancy's Teaching Artist Residency program — local artists transforming the Northwest corner into an open-air studio every Saturday from 1 to 3 p.m., engaging the community across all ages and skill levels. ERIE — MUSEUM extends this existing tradition into a **permanent**, **two-tier-commercial**, **all-weather-rotational** infrastructure that pays artists and runs year-round. The park already wants this; the model fits the place.

Other inaugural sites identified for 2027 expansion: **Tompkins Square Park** (Lower East Side, Saturday-morning audience overlap), **Prospect Park's Long Meadow** (Brooklyn, family-and-stroller dense), **Mission Dolores Park** (San Francisco), **Echo Park Lake** (Los Angeles), **Hyde Park's Speakers' Corner** (London, the historical free-expression precedent that ERIE — MUSEUM is, in spirit, the visual-arts continuation of), **Yoyogi Park** (Tokyo), and **Tiergarten** (Berlin). Each city has a park that already does some of this work informally. The brand makes it formal, durable, and economically sustainable for the artist.

## IV. The Two-Tier Product Architecture

Every artist station carries two products simultaneously. The split is the model's most important commercial mechanism.

**Tier I — The Original.** A one-of-one work made by the artist, valued by the artist, priced by the artist, and sold by the artist directly. The artist keeps 100% of Tier I revenue. ERIE — MUSEUM takes nothing from the original. This is non-negotiable and it is the moral center of the model. The original belongs to the maker. Anyone trying to take a piece of the maker's primary work has already broken the trust the model is built on.

The original is priced at the level the artist sets. A photograph might be $80. A small framed painting might be $350. A hand-bound book might be $120. A medium oil-on-canvas might be $1,800. A large sculpture might be $4,500. The artist sets the number based on materials, time, demand, and pride. ERIE — MUSEUM provides a price-suggestion conversation but no enforcement.

The original carries an **Artwork Passport** — a small QR code printed on the back, on the certificate of authenticity, or on a discreet hangtag, registered onchain via the ERIE Labs registry. The Passport carries the artist's name, the work's title, the date of creation, the materials, the dimensions, the sale date, the buyer's wallet (or a hashed receipt for non-onchain buyers), and any future resale royalties the artist wishes to attach (10% is the default, settable up to 25%). The Passport is permanent. It travels with the work. If the work is resold ten years from now at auction, the original artist receives the royalty automatically.

**Tier II — The Companion.** A wearable, carryable, or hangable piece — t-shirt, hoodie, tote, cap, scarf, sticker pack, art print, pin, patch, ceramic mug — that incorporates the artist's image, signature, or co-designed motif. The companion is co-developed by ERIE Labs (which handles materials, manufacturing, distribution, and quality) and the artist (who supplies the artwork and approves every iteration). The companion is priced in a published architecture from $24 to $180.

The companion exists to solve a real problem. Most park visitors will love the original but cannot, that afternoon, take home an original. The original is too expensive, too fragile, too large, or simply not the visitor's medium. Without a Tier II product, the encounter ends with a thanks-and-goodbye and the artist has earned nothing. With a Tier II product, the visitor leaves with something that carries the encounter into their week. The artist earns a royalty. The work — through the wearer — finds an audience the original could never reach.

**The royalty split on Tier II is 40% to the artist** as the standing baseline, paid on every unit sold, in perpetuity, traceable through the same Artwork Passport infrastructure. This is approximately twice the industry-standard royalty in licensed apparel partnerships and roughly four times what the artist would earn from a typical museum-shop reproduction. The 40% figure is published, public, and unchangeable except upward. (Special collaborations can negotiate higher splits — 50% or 60% — for established artists; the floor never moves down.)

ERIE Labs, in exchange for its 60%, handles the work the artist doesn't want to do: blank apparel sourcing (Foundation-grade Giza-87 cotton, GOTS-certified organic when available, all from the same supply chain that the lifestyle house uses), printing (DTG and screen-printing partners in Brooklyn and Long Island City), embroidery (a single Williamsburg embroiderer), packaging, online catalog, in-park fulfillment (a small mobile point-of-sale that the artist can use without a phone or a Square account), shipping, customer service, returns, sales tax, accounting, royalty distribution, and the Artwork Passport onchain registration. The artist focuses on the work. The lab focuses on the infrastructure.

A note on why 40% specifically: the number is the result of an actual unit-economics calculation. A $58 t-shirt has approximately $14 in blank-and-printing cost, $4 in fulfillment, $2 in payment processing, $2 in retail-overhead amortization, $1 in returns reserve, leaving $35 in gross margin. A 40% artist royalty on the $58 retail is $23.20 — which leaves the lab with $11.80 per unit in net contribution to platform overhead. Across the volume the model expects (one well-attended Saturday produces 50–150 t-shirt sales per artist station), this works for both sides without any other financial instrument.

## V. The ERIE Labs Symbiosis

ERIE Labs is the operating partner. It is not a museum board. It is not a gallery. It is not a label. It is a service-and-infrastructure operation that exists to make the artist's life easier and the visitor's encounter richer.

**What ERIE Labs does:**

1. **Identifies and onboards artists.** The selection criterion is not pedigree, art-world credentialing, gallery representation, or social-media following. The criterion is *the work plus the willingness to be present*. Open submissions are reviewed every two months; selections are made by a rotating panel of three current artists plus two ERIE Labs staff. The onboarding process is a single 90-minute conversation, a portfolio review, and a one-page agreement.

2. **Schedules the rotation.** Each Saturday in Washington Square hosts four artists at four stations across the park's permitted zones. Each artist is scheduled for one Saturday per month at a minimum and up to four Saturdays per month maximum. The schedule is published 12 weeks in advance.

3. **Provides the infrastructure.** Folding tables, weather-rated canopies, branded but minimal signage, a mobile point-of-sale, a payment terminal, a roll of receipt paper, packaging materials, weather contingency plans, restroom access protocols, and a safety contact. The artist arrives with their work and leaves with their work. Everything else is the lab's responsibility.

4. **Manufactures and distributes the Tier II companions.** Every artist who joins the program co-designs at least one companion garment within their first three months. Manufacturing runs are small — 80 units to 400 units per design — and use the Foundation supply chain. Inventory is held at the lab's Long Island City fulfillment space. Companions are sold both in-park (at the artist station, on the day) and online (through the ERIE Labs catalog, year-round).

5. **Pays the artist.** Payments are processed weekly. Tier I sales pay out the day of (the lab's payment terminal sends funds directly to the artist's account; the lab takes 0%). Tier II sales pay out weekly via direct deposit or stablecoin (the artist chooses).

6. **Operates the Artwork Passport registry.** Every Tier I work and every Tier II design is registered onchain through the ERIE Labs registry — built on the Zora V3 royalty-standard infrastructure for primary-market and resale-royalty enforcement, with provenance metadata that follows the work through every subsequent owner. The registry is public, queryable, and free for the artist to use forever.

7. **Holds the legal infrastructure.** Insurance, permits, vendor agreements, sales-tax collection, the operational paperwork that no individual artist should ever have to deal with. The lab carries it.

8. **Runs the community programming.** Once a month, an evening at the Long Island City fulfillment space — drinks, food, a talk by a current artist, an open critique session, a chance for artists to meet each other, the audience, and the lab. The programming converts a transactional model into a relational one.

**What ERIE Labs explicitly does not do:**

- Curate taste in the artist's primary work. The artist makes what the artist makes.
- Take a percentage of Tier I sales. Ever.
- Sign artists to exclusive contracts. The artist is free to also show in galleries, sell on their own website, license to other apparel makers, exhibit in museums.
- Pressure the artist toward commercial themes. If an artist wants to make work that won't sell, they make work that won't sell.
- Hide the artist behind the brand. The artist's name is on every Tier II garment. The artist's signature is in the print. The artist's bio is on the website. The artist is the work; the lab is the infrastructure.

## VI. The ERIE — MUSEUM vs. The Existing Order

The model is best understood by what it is not.

**vs. The institutional museum (Met, MoMA, Tate, Pompidou, Whitney, Guggenheim).**

The institutional museum holds approximately 2–4% of its collection on view at any given moment; the rest is in storage. ERIE — MUSEUM holds 100% of its current programming on view at any given moment. The institutional museum mediates the artist through a curator, a wall label, a date, and a frame; ERIE — MUSEUM puts the artist next to the work. The institutional museum charges $25–$30 admission; ERIE — MUSEUM charges $0. The institutional museum's average visitor encounter is approximately 28 seconds per work (well-documented in visitor-attention studies); ERIE — MUSEUM's average encounter is the duration of a conversation, often 4–10 minutes. The institutional museum's gift shop returns approximately 4% of revenue to artists if the artist is alive and licensed (and 0% if the artist is dead or not licensed); ERIE — MUSEUM returns 40%–60% on Tier II and 100% on Tier I.

What the institutional museum does well: scholarship, conservation, scale, historical depth, climate-controlled preservation, the inheritance and reinterpretation of canon. ERIE — MUSEUM does none of these things. It is not a replacement. It is a complement — the working layer that lives upstream of the institutional layer.

**vs. The commercial gallery (Pace, Gagosian, Hauser & Wirth, David Zwirner; mid-tier and emerging galleries).**

The commercial gallery typically takes 50% of primary-market sales and a smaller cut of secondary. Its audience is curated and small; the goal is the collector and the institution. The artist is represented; the gallery decides who gets to see the work, when, and at what price. ERIE — MUSEUM takes 0% of primary-market Tier I sales. Its audience is whoever walks through the park. The artist is present, not represented. The audience can include the collector and the institution but does not require them.

What the commercial gallery does well: international placement, art-fair access, museum-acquisition relationships, scholarly catalogues, market-making. ERIE — MUSEUM does none of these things either. An artist on the ERIE — MUSEUM platform is free to also be represented by a gallery; the two operations occupy different points in the artist's economic stack.

**vs. The art fair (Art Basel, Frieze, Armory, NADA).**

The art fair is the gallery system compressed into four days inside a convention center. Booth fees range from $20,000 to $250,000 per fair. The audience is collectors, advisors, critics, and members. The art fair is the antithesis of the public park: it is filtered admission, exclusive access, transactional density, and a clock. ERIE — MUSEUM is the inversion — open admission, broad access, relational time, and a calendar that runs every Saturday for the next decade.

**vs. The street fair, the artisan market, the craft fair (Smorgasburg art-side, Bushwick Open Studios, Brooklyn Flea, Renegade Craft, Maker Faire).**

The street fair is the closest existing analog. It has the open-air, the artist-presence, the price accessibility, and the social mix. What it lacks is the **infrastructure** — the Artwork Passport, the royalty plumbing, the Tier I/Tier II separation, the ongoing rotation rather than one-off events, and the symbiotic lab partner that lifts the artist out of solo small-business operating costs. ERIE — MUSEUM is what happens when the street fair acquires the operational backbone of a small museum and the technological backbone of a 2026 onchain creator platform.

**vs. The outdoor sculpture park (Storm King, Dia:Beacon, Crystal Bridges, MASS MoCA, Naoshima, Inhotim).**

The outdoor sculpture park is a permanent placement of major works on landscape. It is the most beautiful version of the institutional museum — but it is institutional. The artists are not present except at openings. The work is selected by curators. Admission is paid. The sculpture park trades duration for openness; it is closer to land art than to a working market. ERIE — MUSEUM trades the opposite way: less permanence, more openness, the artist always present.

**vs. The NFT marketplace (OpenSea, Foundation, SuperRare, Zora, Manifold).**

The NFT marketplace solves provenance and royalty enforcement extraordinarily well — Zora V3's royalty-standard contracts pay artists onchain on every secondary sale, instantly, with no waiting period; Manifold's creator-contract architecture gives artists full sovereignty over their own minting infrastructure. What it does not solve is the human encounter. The NFT buyer never meets the artist. The work never has a body. The audience never has a Saturday afternoon. ERIE — MUSEUM uses the NFT layer as plumbing — the Artwork Passport rides on it — without making the platform itself a screen-mediated experience. The artist is in the park. The collector receives a physical work. The blockchain quietly handles the resale-royalty problem in the background and never appears in the visitor's awareness.

**vs. Burning Man and the festival circuit.**

Burning Man and adjacent festivals (Lightning in a Bottle, Afropunk, Outside Lands, Beyond Wonderland) produce extraordinary art experiences but on a once-a-year, ticketed-entry, geographically-remote basis. The audience is a self-selected subculture. ERIE — MUSEUM is the opposite: every Saturday, free entry, in the city center, audience is everyone.

**vs. The teaching-artist program (the Washington Square Park Conservancy's existing Saturday program; municipal arts programs in major cities).**

The teaching-artist program is the spiritual ancestor of ERIE — MUSEUM. The Conservancy's Saturday-1-to-3-p.m. residency runs in the same Northwest corner of the same park, with the same open-air-public-encounter ethos. ERIE — MUSEUM extends it: the teaching program is grant-funded, hours-bounded, and demonstration-focused; the museum is commercial-but-artist-favorable, year-round, and product-architectured. The two could coexist in the same park on the same day. The teaching program is the morning. The museum is the afternoon.

## VII. The Encounter Is the Work

The model's deepest claim is that **the encounter between artist and audience is itself the artistic medium**. The painting is the trace. The garment is the souvenir. The conversation in the park — the question the visitor asks, the answer the artist gives, the small adjustment to the visitor's interior life that happens when they hear it — is the actual work being made.

This is not romanticism. It is closer to the philosophical position that every gathered situation produces a *new* thing that did not previously exist — a position descended from Whitehead's *actual occasions*, Buber's *I-Thou*, Cage's *4'33"* understanding that the audience and the air complete the composition, and the relational-aesthetics tradition (Bourriaud, *Esthétique Relationnelle*, 1998) which argued that art's true medium in the contemporary moment is human relations themselves.

ERIE — MUSEUM commits to the position. The garment carries the encounter; the original anchors it; the park hosts it; the lab makes it possible. The encounter is the work.

A consequence: every garment in the Tier II catalog is, in this view, an instrument for **carrying high-density information forward in time**. The wearer leaves the park with a t-shirt that contains, encoded in its image and memory, the entire afternoon they spent meeting the artist. Three months later, when they put the shirt on, the afternoon comes back. Three years later, when they wear it on a different city's street and a stranger comments on it, the encounter propagates again. The garment is, in the language of the broader ERI corpus, a high-Fisher-information object — a small physical token that carries an unusual amount of meaningful, per-unit-mass information about a specific time and place and human exchange.

Most apparel does not work this way. Most apparel is information-poor: a logo, a graphic, a brand. The wearer's relationship to the garment is mediated by the brand's marketing rather than by a personal memory. ERIE — MUSEUM's Tier II is the inverse. Every garment is a personal artifact disguised as a fashion item. The artist's name on the inside label is not branding. It is the receipt for an afternoon.

## VIII. We Wear Our Ideas, And Our Ideas Wear Us

The line is the operating philosophy of the entire ERIE family. Stated long-form:

What we put on our bodies signals what we believe is true, what we are loyal to, what we want to carry forward, what we want others to know without having to say it. Clothing is, before fashion, a non-verbal language. It is a way of declaring — to ourselves first and to others second — what we are with. We wear our ideas.

And we are, in turn, shaped by what we wear. The garment changes our posture, our gait, our sense of what room we belong in. Over months, the daily decision of what we put on becomes a daily decision about what kind of person we are practicing being. The garment is a small ritual technology that quietly trains the wearer into themselves. Our ideas wear us.

ERIE — MUSEUM's Tier II garments are designed for this two-way traffic. The wearer chooses the artist they want to carry. The artist's image, once worn, slowly does its work on the wearer — turning them, week by week, into the kind of person who is at home in conversation with that artist's vision. This is not a metaphor. It is the actual mechanism by which apparel does its cultural work, observable across every subculture in the history of dress, from the 1960s peace patches to the 1990s Stüssy logo to the 2010s Supreme box logo to the 2020s Aimé Leon Dore cap. The garment trains its wearer.

The model takes responsibility for this. The artists ERIE Labs partners with are chosen, in part, for the *kind of carrying* their work asks of the wearer. Work that asks for warmth produces wearers who become warmer. Work that asks for precision produces wearers who become more precise. Work that asks for joy produces wearers who become more joyful. The lab does not select for ideology. The lab selects for *effect on the wearer*.

## IX. Evidence for an Emergent Reality Intelligence Ecosystem

A side claim of the model: a sufficiently dense, decentralized, recurrent network of artist-audience encounters in public space produces, over time, observable evidence of *emergent collective intelligence*. The park, week after week, becomes a living distributed cognitive substrate. Ideas that are not yet legible at the individual level become legible at the network level. New movements, new aesthetic vocabularies, new forms of cross-cultural synthesis emerge from the Saturday-after-Saturday accumulation of small encounters.

The framing draws on contemporary research into emergent intelligence in distributed systems — the same research line that informs how foundation models trained on large human-text corpora develop capabilities that no individual training datum contains. The park is a foundation model whose training data is human encounter. Each Saturday is one training step. Each artist-audience exchange is one rank-one update to the cumulative integrated information of the system. After 200 Saturdays, the park has accumulated a measurable, non-trivial layer of shared cognition that did not previously exist.

This is testable. The lab will, beginning in 2026, publish an annual report — *Field Notes from the Park* — documenting:

1. **Cross-artist collaboration emergence rate.** How often do artists who have never met begin co-producing work after sharing the park on the same day for some number of Saturdays?
2. **Audience-to-artist conversion rate.** How often does someone who first encountered the museum as a visitor return as a participating maker?
3. **Aesthetic-vocabulary diffusion.** Can the field-team identify motifs, color languages, or formal techniques that originated with one artist on the platform and migrated, organically, to others over a measurable number of months?
4. **Wearer-network density.** Tier II garments leave the park and travel on bodies. The Artwork Passport tracks the rough geographic distribution of where the garments end up. Over time, the diffusion map becomes visible.
5. **Cross-format spillover.** Park work that migrates into formal galleries, museum acquisitions, residencies, fellowships, public-art commissions, brand collaborations — the secondary career outcomes that come from the Saturday infrastructure.

The Field Notes annual is the lab's research instrument and its public commitment to falsifiability. If the model is generating the kind of emergent collective cognition the philosophy claims, the report will show it. If not, the report will show that too, and the model will be revised in the open.

## X. The Operational Calendar

ERIE — MUSEUM operates on a yearly calendar. Each year has the following structure.

**January.** Quiet month. The lab spends January in Long Island City, in production for the spring Tier II catalog. Artists submit new work for review. The Field Notes annual is published on January 15th.

**February — March.** Cold-weather rotation in Washington Square — fewer artists per Saturday (two stations rather than four), a smaller footprint, an emphasis on indoor-ready work (prints, books, ceramics, fiber) over weather-vulnerable mediums. The Saturday hours move to 12–3 p.m. to use the warmest part of the day.

**April.** First full-rotation Saturday of the year. Four stations, full operation. The April catalog drops both online and in-park.

**May — September.** High season. Four stations every Saturday. Sundays added in July and August. Average attendance: 800–2,500 visitors per Saturday across the four stations. The audience grows through summer.

**October.** Fall harvest of Tier I work. Many artists save their best summer work for October sales — the audience is denser, the light is better for photography, the museum's digital catalog gets its strongest year-round visit through October's Instagram and Substack momentum.

**November.** Thanksgiving Saturday is the year's largest single day. The lab opens a small temporary indoor extension — the **Winter Atelier** — in a Long Island City warehouse for any Saturday when the park is unworkable. The Atelier carries the same artists, the same Tier I/Tier II split, the same ethos, indoors.

**December.** The Winter Atelier carries through December. The park returns to two-station rotation on warmer Saturdays. The annual *Field Notes* is finalized in December, published in mid-January.

The discipline of the calendar is the same as the lifestyle house: predictable, restrained, no surprises. Artists know when they will be working. Audiences know when to come. The infrastructure compounds.

## XI. The Artist's Economic Path

A working sketch of what an artist on the platform makes in a representative year — not a guarantee, but the realistic median across the program's first cohort:

| Income source | Annual estimate |
| --- | --- |
| Tier I primary-market sales (28 Saturdays at $400 average original-sale day) | $11,200 |
| Tier II royalty (40% on $24,000 in collaborative apparel sales) | $9,600 |
| Tier I resale royalties (10% on three secondary sales averaging $1,500) | $450 |
| Programming honoraria (one talk evening, one workshop) | $800 |
| **Median annual artist income from ERIE — MUSEUM** | **~$22,050** |

This is not a living wage in New York. It is also not nothing. For an artist whose work would otherwise be priced into invisibility, $22,000 per year — earned in 28 Saturdays of work, supplementing whatever else the artist does — is a meaningful contribution to a working life in art. Top-decile artists on the platform earn 4–6× the median. Bottom-decile artists earn approximately half. The lab publishes the full distribution annually, anonymized.

The medium-term ambition is clear: as the model expands across cities and Saturdays, the median rises. By 2030, the lab targets a median of $60,000–$90,000 per artist per year across a multi-city platform with 250+ artists. This is the level at which the model becomes a primary income for working artists, not a supplement.

## XII. The Visitor's Path

A first Saturday at the park looks like this:

You walk into Washington Square. A small, simple sign — Erie Black with the brand mark, no taller than a person's waist — at the Northwest corner says *ERIE — MUSEUM // Saturday Hours 11–6 // Today's Artists: Polaris, Sable, Lumen, Thalo*. (The artists' real names are on their stations; *Polaris/Sable/Lumen/Thalo* are the rotating zone names.) Each station is a folding table, a canopy, a chair, a small inventory of original work, a small inventory of Tier II garments hanging on a single rail, a payment terminal, an artist.

You walk up to a station. The artist looks up. They say hello. You look at the work. The work is something you've never seen before — a series of small ink drawings of birds in flight, a hand-bound book of poems written in two languages, a series of ceramic vessels glazed in a Wave Indigo that catches the late-afternoon light. You ask about it. The artist tells you. The conversation is three minutes or twelve minutes. You ask the price of one of the drawings. The artist tells you: $140. You think about it. You don't have $140 today. The artist points to a t-shirt on the rail — the same drawing, smaller, printed on a heavyweight Foundation cotton tee, $58. You buy the shirt. You leave with the shirt and the artist's card. Their Instagram. Their next Saturday. The drawing is still there for next time.

Three months later you come back. The drawing is gone — sold to someone else — but the artist is back, with new work. You buy the new drawing. You wear the t-shirt that day. The artist remembers you. You are no longer a visitor. You are part of the audience the platform has been quietly training itself to hold.

This is the model.

## XIII. The 2026–2030 Trajectory

**2026.** Inaugural year. Washington Square Park, every Saturday April–November. 24 artists in the rotation. ~280 Tier I works sold. ~12,000 Tier II garments sold. ~$680,000 in total platform revenue, of which ~$400,000 flows to artists. The Long Island City fulfillment space opens in March. The first Winter Atelier opens in November. The first *Field Notes* publishes January 2027.

**2027.** Expansion to Tompkins Square Park (Saturdays) and Prospect Park's Long Meadow (Sundays). The artist roster grows to 60. Total platform revenue reaches ~$2.4M; artist payouts reach ~$1.4M. The Field Notes annual is read by 40,000 subscribers. The lab opens a satellite fulfillment in Bushwick.

**2028.** Expansion to one West Coast city (Mission Dolores Park, San Francisco) and one international (Yoyogi Park, Tokyo, October–November pilot). The artist roster grows to 140. Total platform revenue reaches ~$8M; artist payouts reach ~$4.6M. The first ERIE — MUSEUM monograph is published — a hardcover photographic record of the platform's first three years.

**2029.** The model becomes the standard. Five cities active (NY, SF, LA-Echo Park, Tokyo, Berlin-Tiergarten). 200+ artists. The first museum acquisition of an ERIE-discovered artist's work into a major institutional collection (target: a single Whitney Biennial-eligible work in 2029 cycle). The Artwork Passport infrastructure is open-sourced and offered to other artist-platforms.

**2030.** Five-year retrospective exhibition at a single major museum — likely the Studio Museum in Harlem or the Brooklyn Museum, conversations underway. The retrospective is **inside** the institutional museum, but its content is **the park** — a documentary-and-archive show about what the museum looks like when the museum is the park. The model has, by then, demonstrated its second-order claim: the institutional museum and the decentralized park-museum are not in competition. They are stages in a continuous economic and cultural circuit, and the park-museum is the working layer that feeds the institutional layer everything fresh.

## XIV. The Closing

A museum, in the Greek sense, is not a building. It is wherever the Muses are present. The Muses are present wherever a maker and an audience meet under attention.

Washington Square has been doing this informally for a hundred years. ERIE — MUSEUM is the formalization, the infrastructure, the economic plumbing, the technological back-end, and the brand language that lets the informal tradition compound into a durable, artist-favorable, year-round, publicly-accessible institution.

The art is the interaction. The interaction is the art.

The original belongs to the artist. The companion carries the encounter forward. The lab handles the infrastructure. The park hosts the gathering. The visitor walks home wearing a piece of an afternoon they will not forget.

We wear our ideas, and our ideas wear us.

The encounter is the work.

The park is the gallery.
